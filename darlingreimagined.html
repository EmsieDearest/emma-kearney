The Art of Accessibility

SCOPE

I've been a loyal reader of Darling Magazine since discovering its first issue while in the checkout line at Whole Foods. Their philosophy was written so eloquently on the back cover. It reminded me of the values my feminist mother instilled in me: that what I do means so much more than how I look doing it. I can be a fierce go-getter in my career and invest in my future. I can fight for the rights of others and still be a feminine woman. I have mad respect for the magazine and the thoughtfully designed spreads. I think their UI is fantastic and wouldn't change a thing.

PROBLEM

That being said, the development lacked basic accessibility criteria. The navigation had major issues, buttons lacked discernible text, ID values were indistinguishable, form elements lacked proper labeling, links had the same name and were indistinguishable. When tabbing through the page, improper syntax was being utilized. Any vague span and div tags needed ARIA labels, there needed to be a skip nav, the landmarks on the page needed to be evident. Alt tags were missing on photos. The tabbing order needed to be more intuitive. Some sections of the page were inaccessible through keyboard use. I could go on. The test I ran found over 300 issues preventing an intuitive screen reader experience. It's like the web equivalent of not making the front door of a building wheelchair accessible. This was a major problem. Basically anyone with blindness, paralysis and other kinds of disabilities could not use this site. They're setting themselves up for a class-action lawsuit. I don't mean to make Darling look bad. Most stakeholders do not have this on their radar. This is why I specialize in it. To make the web more accessible for all.

SOLUTION

The development needed an overhaul. For the purpose of not violating any copyright laws, I redesigned an article to conceptually show how this would look if it met WCAG AA compliance. Scroll down to the video.
